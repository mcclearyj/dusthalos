\section{Executive Summary}

Over the last year or so, here is what I have been able to do: 
\begin{enumerate}
\item Identified some interesting literature results that support our findings; see Section \ref{sec:literature} for details. Briefly, it appears that our $A_V$ signal has gone from ``anomalous reddening'' to accepted fact about the circumgalactic medium.
\item Significantly refactored the analysis code repository, making it more modular and robust. The lion's share of my efforts were spent here but this also proving to be a worthwhile investment. See Section \ref{sec:code}.
\item Weeded out numerous bugs in the original calculation, such as incorrectly scaling the M\'{e}nard (2010) $A_V$ curve when creating output plots and improper handling of survey masks defined in galactic coordinates. 
\item Calculated the extended reddening signal using WISExSCOS and the DES Y3 GOLD and redMaGiC catalogs; my first results were based on DES Y1. Rest assured, our extended reddening signal hasn't disappeared. Details are in Section \ref{sec:results}.
\item Submitted an abstract to an Aspen Center for Physics Winter Conference on dust \url{https://astro.ufl.edu/aspendust2024/}. It was accepted and I am giving a talk :) (Section \ref{sec:abstract}). 
\end{enumerate}

The new extended reddening profiles, computed using the SuperCOSMOSxWISE as a foreground catalog and the two DES Y3 redMaGiC catalogs for backgrounds, are shown in Figures \ref{fig:profile_zbins}. The signal is very much still there, which is great, and does not appear to change with the choice of dust model (Figure \ref{fig:profile_dustmodels}). An additional, cool finding shown in Figure \ref{fig:lrg_halo} is the confirmation of excess reddening for luminous red galaxies, a result supported by the literature (Figure \ref{fig:literature}). 

There are a couple of findings that are bothering me, and on which I would appreciate your insight. First, as the number of redshift bins is increased, the dust extinction signal measured with redMaGiC high-luminosity, high-redshift galaxies all but disappears (Figure \ref{fig:profile_zbins}), even though Figure \ref{fig:avhist} shows that the distribution of $A_V$ becomes decreasingly biased as the number of bins increases. The strong detection of dust from redMaGiC LRGs suggests that it \textit{is} possible to extract reddening signal from the high-redshift sample, so I am not entirely sure about why this happens with the WISExSCOS foreground. 

Second, there are significant differences in the shape and amplitude of the ``raw,'' uncorrected cross-correlation signal and the fully-random-subtracted signal. I decided to examine the relative contributions of each term in the cross-correlation estimator of Equation \ref{eqn:xi_estimator}. Figure \ref{fig:fgr_vs_not} shows that the random-foreground/real-background cross-correlation term is driving the change and may be adding spurious signal at large impact parameters. However, foreground subtraction actually gets rid of this extra signal in the LRG extinction (Figure \ref{fig:lrg_halo}). So, I am bemused by this result too. 

All that said, we have established some encouraging initial (re-)detections, which is great! There are now a few tasks to complete. Further null testing of the estimator is in order, as is fixing the variances in the $A_V$ correlation functions (I am using the default, which is an underestimate). I am also planning on expanding this analysis to catalogs beyond redMaGiC. An obvious candidate is the DES MAGLIM sample, which I will soon set up. I would also like to revisit the GSWLC (GALEX-SDSS-WISE Legacy Catalog) and  IIFSCz sample (IRAS 60 micron observations) catalogs for foregrounds. Even better would be the eBOSS sample used in Lan \& Mo (2018), which would allow us to replicate their Mg II absorber result in extinction and provide a connection to dust surface mass density and halo mass. 

With these results, we could investigate $A_V$ as a function of galaxy properties such as color (at least red sequence vs. blue sequence) and specific star formation rate. Section \ref{sec:next_steps} lists some other, more interesting analyses we could do, too. Regardless, we should probably start thinking about a paper. \textbf{If you have complementary results, particularly of dust in emission, it would be great to include them with our $A_V$ results!}
 
In the rest of this little report, I will cover these points in greater depth. Sections \ref{sec:results} and \ref{sec:next_steps} are what I consider to be the most important; if you are pressed for time, you can probably skip the rest.

\section{Reminder: optimal estimation and cross-correlation calculation}\label{sec:theory}

In Eric's original idea, an estimator for excess (non-cosmological) reddening of light from background galaxies could be derived by maximizing the likelihood 

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:likelihood}
\log L = \rm \left [\mathbf{D} - \mathbf{M}(\rho)\right ] \frac{\mathbf{C}^{-1}}{2} \left[\mathbf{D} - \mathbf{M}(\rho)\right ]^{\rm T}
\end{equation}

where the data vector $\mathbf{D}$ is an array of magnitudes, e.g.,

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:data_vector}
\mathbf{\rm D} = \{\rm g,r,i,z,...\}
\end{equation}

and the covariance of the data $\mathbf{C}$ encapsulates both the magnitude measurement uncertainties and cosmic variance.

The model $\mathbf{M}(\rho)$ describes the data $\mathbf{D}$ in terms of a parameter $\rho$ (here, a unit $A_V$); the model can be Taylor-expanded to obtain a simple linear approximation for the effect of $\rho$:
\begin{equation}\label{model}
\rm \mathbf{M} = M_0 + \rho\ \dMdp
\end{equation}
 
In this expansion, $M_0$ are the intrinsic colors of galaxies in the background galaxy sample, without any reddening from dust. The $\rho$ \dMinline~ term is the response of galaxies to a unit dust; \dMinline~is a fiducial (theoretical) extinction curve for dust.

The optimal estimator $\hat{\rho}$ for the dust extinction, such that $\rho$ maximizes the likelihood in Equation~\ref{eqn:likelihood}, is given as: 

\begin{equation}
\hat{\rho} = \rm \left[ \frac{ \dMdp \ \mathbf{C}^{-1} \left (\mathbf{D} - M_0 \right )} {\dMdp\ \mathbf{C}^{-1 }\ \dMdp} \right ] \label{eqn:estimator}
\end{equation}

where by the Cram\'{e}r-Rao bound, the uncertainty on $\hat{\rho}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
\sigma^2_{\hat{\rho}} = \rm \dMdp\ \mathbf{C}^{-1 }\ \dMdp \label{eqn:RCO}
\end{equation}

A slight complication to Equation \ref{eqn:estimator} is introduced by the redshift dependence of the average galaxy color $\overline{M_0}$. In practice, the quantity $\hat{\rho}$ must be estimated in bins of galaxy redshift, with the exact number of bins chosen to balance the variance and change of $\overline{M_0}$ as a function of redshift. The effect of different redhsift bins is explored in Section \ref{sec:results}.   

With the estimator $\hat{\rho}$ in place, foreground galaxy number counts may be cross-correlated with the dust-induced extinction of 
background galaxies. Denoting $N(\theta_1)$ as the galaxy count at a position $\theta_1$ on the sky and $A_V(\theta_2)$ as the 
extinction at position $\theta_2$, the cross-correlation may be expressed as the average deviations of 
$N$ and $A_V$ from their mean values at two sky positions $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ separated by $\theta$:

\begin{equation}
\xi_{N A_V}(\theta) = \left< [N(\theta_1) - \overline{N}]\,[A_V(\theta_2) - \overline{M_0}(z)] \right> \label{eqn:correl}
\end{equation}

The mean values, $\overline{N}$ and $\overline{M_0}(z)$, are the average (foreground) galaxy count and average color of (background) galaxies at redshift z, respectively, over the entire survey area. We compute $\xi_{N A_V}(\theta)$ using galaxy-kappa cross-correlation method provided in the \texttt{TreeCorr} package\footnote{https://github.com/rmjarvis/TreeCorr}. Though intended to correlate galaxy counts with shear signal (i.e., $\kappa$) the code is flexible enough to accommodate any scalar quantity such as $A_V$.

\textcolor{red}{Could we double-check this estimator? Does that $R_N D_{A_V}$ actually need to be there? Also, should there be a factor of $1/\xi_{\rm rr}$? Though if I add one the plot blows up...}
As with any such analysis, the contribution of random correlations, selection biases, etc. must be taken into account. We use the following estimator for the true galaxy-reddening cross-correlation $\xi_{N A_V}(\theta)$:
\begin{equation}
\xi_{N A_V}(\theta) = \frac{D_N D_{A_V}(\theta) - D_N R_{A_V}(\theta) - R_N D_{A_V}(\theta) + R_N R_{A_V}(\theta)}{R_N R_{A_V}(\theta)}\label{eqn:xi_estimator}
\end{equation}

$D_N D_{A_V}$ is the signal from the correlation of the real foreground galaxy and background color catalogs; $D_N R_{A_V}$ and $R_N D_{A_V}$ are the correlations of real foreground with random background catalog and random foreground with real background catalogs, respectively; and $R_N R_{A_V}$ is the correlation of the random foreground and background catalogs.

Figure 2 from that paper and its caption are reproduced below in Figure \ref{fig:literature}. That paper also establishes some potentially fruitful lines of investigation, such as calculating extinction for red versus blue galaxies and even as a function of star formation rate if we can get estimates from, e.g.,

\section{Catalogs}\label{sec:catalogs}
The results presented here are made using the WISExSCOS photometric redshift catalog as a foreground and the Dark Energy Survey redMaGiC catalogs (Rozo et al.~2016) as backgrounds. 
% Cite Pandey et al: https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2022PhRvD.106d3520P/abstract 
The Y3 cosmology analyses divided the redMaGiC galaxies into two catalogs across five tomographic bins from $0.15 < z < 0.90$. Galaxies in the first three redshift bins ($0.15 < z < 0.65$) were drawn from the ``high density'' catalog, which is volume limited to a z-band luminosity of 0.5L*. The last two tomographic bins ($0.65 < z < 0.90$) are drawn from the ``high luminosity'' catalog, which is volume limited to a z-band luminosity of 1.0L*. Because the galaxy population and selection functions differ between the two samples, I perform separate analyses on the high-density and high-redshift redMaGiC catalogs. 

The redMaGiC catalogs do not contain photometry and must be joined with another catalog to get it. The following query is used to obtain a relatively pure sample of DES Y3 GOLD galaxies (Sevilla-Noarbe et al.~2021), which can be used both for augmenting redMaGiC galaxies with photometry and separate investigations:

\begin{verbatim}
    flags_footprint = 1 AND flags_foreground = 0 AND (flags_gold & 1111110) = 0 
    AND (flags_ badregions & 110) = 0 AND extended_class_mash_mof = 3  
\end{verbatim}

The Y3 GOLD catalog provides several different photometric measures. Our analysis uses the \texttt{\{g,r,i,z\}\_mof\_cm\_mag\_corrected} magnitudes. The multi-object-fitting composite model (MOF CM) flux measurements are based on a Gaussian mixture model that accounts for the light profiles of nearby galaxies with a friends-of-friends algorithm. The \texttt{mof\_cm\_mag\_corrected} magnitudes also account for galactic extinction, color-dependent source spectral energy distributions, etc.

Besides the Y3\_GOLD query reproduced above, the following selections are imposed on the data before the reddening calculation in \texttt{ReddeningCalculator} proceeds:

\begin{verbatim}
    catlen = len(self.data)
    wg = np.full(catlen, True)
    
    for band in bands:
        band_mag = np.ma.getdata(self.data[band])
        band_bool = (band_mag > -9999) & \
                    (band_mag != np.nan) & \
                    (band_mag < 28)
        wg *= band_bool
\end{verbatim}

This cut is quite minor and only eliminates an additional 1\% of galaxies.

The WISExSCOS catalog\footnote{\url{http://ssa.roe.ac.uk/WISExSCOS.html}} derives from the cross-match of mid-infrared Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer catalogs and SuperCOSMOS scans of UKST/POSS-II photographic plates (Bilicki et al.~2016). The full catalog contains 20 million sources and covers the whole sky; this analysis is restricted to the subset of galaxies that overlap with the DES Y3 field and have corrected ANNz photometric redshifts $z < 0.11$. The value of the redshift threshold is based on the 1-$\sigma$ scatter of $z_{\rm zPhoto\_Corr}$, reported by Bilicki et al.~as $\sigma_z = 0.035$; a cut at $z < 0.11$ minimizes overlap with the lowest redshift bin of redMaGiC galaxies ($z > 0.15$) while maintaining an adequately high source density. A further selection of $bCalCorr > 14.5$ reduces stellar contamination. 

Separate random galaxy catalogs are provided for the two redMaGiC galaxy samples, but not for WISExSCOS. To be able to complete the calculation in Equation \ref{eqn:xi_estimator}, I generate a random catalog for WISExSCOS using the method in Section \ref{sec:code:randoms}.

\item 1,774,394 in the redMaGiC high-density galaxy catalog, 
\item 70,581,716 in the redMaGiC high-density random catalog,
\item 819,626 in the redMaGiC high-redshift galaxy catalog,
\item and 32,596,991 galaxies in the redMaGiC high-redshift random catalog.
\end{itemize}

\section{Code flow}\label{sec:code}

As stated above, I have tried to make \texttt{dusthalos} more useable and robust than it was before, interfacing with code using configuration files, eliminating hardcoded numbers and column names, separating runner scripts from class definitions, etc. Relatedly, the code now handles input galaxy catalogs much more reasonably: preprocessing is now handled separately from reddening and correlation calculations (rather than one giant mess to do it all), and any processing steps are recorded and timestamped in the headers of output catalogs. The idea here was to simplify switching in different foreground-background catalogs rather than having to change the dust calculation code each time (!!).   


\subsection{Catalog processing}\label{sec:code:catalogs}
The high-level workflow is: 

 \begin{itemize}
    \item Load in the foreground and background catalogs and their 
    respective HEALpix survey masks.
    \item Apply survey masks to the catalogs, keeping only those galaxies that 
    lie in the intersection of both HEALPix masks. 
    \item If needed, create a random galaxy catalog. 
    \item If needed, join the background redshift catalog to a photometry catalog.
    \item Save to file.
 \end{itemize}
 
Each of these steps is accomplished using runner scripts like \texttt{cat\_prep\_runner.py} and a YAML configuration file, an example of which is shown in Appendix \ref{appdx:cat_config}.

\subsubsection{Mask handling}\label{sec:code:masks}

Survey masks are manipulated with the HpMask class defined in \texttt{hp\_mask.py}. I am quite proud of this class, which manages all HEALPix mask information and operations, including data loading, coordinate transformation, and application of masks to catalogs. Notably, this HpMask class now properly handles the coordinate system of the survey masks and allows for partial masks like those supplied by DES. Though designed for this analysis, I tried to make HpMask is generic enough that it could (hopefully) be used by anyone. 
 
There are two key methods of HpMask:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{apply\_mask(coords, lonlat, vb)}: Applies the mask to a given set of \texttt{SkyCoord} objects. Returns indices of coordinates that overlap with the mask's seen pixels. Provides a verbose output option.
    \item \texttt{apply\_overlapping\_masks(coords, mask1, mask2)}: Static method that returns indices of coordinates lying within the overlapping area of two masks.
\end{itemize}

In the course of my default processing, HpMask is used to apply both foreground and background survey masks to both foreground and background masks. In other words, all catalogs are ``double-masked.''

\subsubsection{Creating random catalogs}\label{sec:code:randoms}

Any analysis that uses two-point correlation functions needs to distinguishing random correlations from the real signal of interest; this is usually accomplished with random catalogs (as you both know better than I). For cases where random catalogs are not available, I developed a child class of HpMask called FgRandom (defined in \texttt{fg\_random.py}). FgRandom uses a seeded random number generator and the survey mask's HEALPix \texttt{NSIDE} parameter to produce random points on the sphere, applies survey mask(s) to the coordinates if desired, and then saves the coordinates to file. 

An example of a random catalog generated with FgRandom is shown in Figure \ref{fig:overlap3}. The moire effect is an artifact of projection, and as far as I can tell, is not present in the catalog itself.

\subsubsection{Adding photometry to background galaxy catalogs}\label{sec:code:photom}

The redMaGiC catalogs (both real and random) have positions and redshifts, but not magnitudes. To obtain 
colors, I join them with their counterparts in the Y3 GOLD catalog on the 
\texttt{COADD\_OBJECT\_ID} parameter. The random galaxy catalog obviously doesn't 
have a corresponding entry in the GOLD catalog so they are assigned photometry from the Y3 GOLD catalog randomly. This might be a bad call, because there is (obviously) a color gradient with redshift (see Figure \ref{fig:colorredshift}). \textcolor{red}{Doing redshift-based matching of randoms is on my to-do list for randoms.} A third option also exists: matching catalogs by astrometry.

\subsection{Extinction and correlation calculation}
As elsewhere, the correlation calculation code takes a modular approach, separating the calculation into distinct classes and functions. The code uses \texttt{TreeCorr} for the actual correlation computations and relies on a series of YAML configuration files to specify parameters, allowing for a high level of customization and reuse.

\subsubsection{Extinction models}
Models for dust-grain-induced reddening are generated with the \texttt{extinction} Python package\footnote{\url{https://extinction.readthedocs.io/en/latest/index.html}}, which presents ``Cython-optimized implementations of empirical dust exitinction laws found in the literature.'' The particular empirical model to used in our $A_V$ measurement is set in the correlation configuration file (see Appendix \ref{appdx:dust_config}); allowable options are \texttt{calzetti00}, \texttt{fm07}, \texttt{odonnell94}, and \texttt{fitzgerald99}. A class called ExtinctionModel, defined in \texttt{extinction\_model.py}, handles all error checking and calculations and stores the per-bandpass extinction in magnitudes for subsequent calculations.

All models require wavelengths at which to compute the extinction. The Virtual Observatory's Filter Profile Service (a thousand blessings be upon them) provide reference wavelengths for the DECam filter set shown in Table \ref{tab:filters}. I chose to calculate the extinction at the reference wavelength $\lambda_{\rm ref}$ for each filter, as it splits the difference between the other two measures. The \texttt{extinction} extinction models also require values of $R_V$ and/or $A_V$ to be set. The values of $R_V$ in the literature fluctuate significantly from galaxy to galaxy (possibly redshift as well), and different $R_V$s will produce different extinction profiles. In the absence of a compelling reason to do otherwise, I kept the default values of $R_V = 3.1$ (Milky Way-type dust) and $A_V = 1$.

\subsection{Reddening and correlation calculation}

The core of this analysis software is a class called ReddeningCalculator (defined in \texttt{reddening\_calculator.py}), which extends ExtinctionModel and handles the reddening calculation, storing the \av maximum likelihood estimates and their variance. The class also preprocesses the catalog to remove entries with problematic values like NaNs and handles cases where certain redshift bins have too few galaxies for reliable color estimation. 

Briefly, the steps are:

\begin{enumerate}

    \item Loop over all redshift bins specified in the configuration file (\texttt{bin\_numbers}) 
    \item Create a boolean mask to select only galaxies in the current redshift bin
    \item Compute \av and the minumum uncertainty on \av for galaxies in the current redshift bin
    \item Assign the reddening and variance to the corresponding entry for the galaxies in the output arrays. 

\end{enumerate}

The reddening calculation iself is defined in ReddeningCalculator.optimal\_estimator() and proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
         \item Extract galaxy magnitudes from the catalog
 
         \item Stack the extracted magnitudes into a 2D array

         \item Compute the inverse covariance matrix of the galaxy magnitudes

         \item Initialize an ``excess reddening'' vector based on the selected dust extinction model

         \item De-mean the galaxy magnitudes so that ``excess reddening'' \av of galaxies in this redshift bin can be measured. 

         \item Compute the optimal estimator for the excess reddening of galaxies \av. 
         \item Compute the Cramer-Rao bound for the optimal estimator. 

\end{enumerate}

The Correlator class is designed to handle the loading and manipulation of catalogs for performing the dust extinction calculations. It acts as a wrapper around TreeCorr's catalog functionalities, with methods for reading in a galaxy catalog and prepping it for processing with TreeCorr, runs the reddening calculation, and saves the final catalog to a file. 

The entry point for these calculations is a script called \texttt{dust\_calc\_runner.py}, which orchestrates the entire flow. It reads the correlation configuration from a YAML file (Appendix \ref{appdx:dust_config}) and creates the directories if they don't exist.
It then loads both the foreground and background catalogs (real and randoms) as Correlator class instances and then executes the reddening calculations. A method called get\_dust() executes the cross-correlations between real and random catalogs of foreground and background galaxies using TreeCorr then saves the correlation objects to file. Finally, using the \texttt{plot\_res()} method of the Plotter class (defined in \texttt{plotter.py}), a nice plot of the extinction curve is saved to file.  

An example output figure is shown in Figure \ref{fig:extinction}.

\section{Initial results}\label{sec:results}

Figures and tables in this section summarize the results of the $A_V$ estimation with the the two redMaGiC galaxy catalogs and the cross-correlation of \av with galaxy counts in WISExSCOS. It is encouraging that extinction is clearly detected out to 10 Mpc with both redMaGiC samples and their respective redshift binning schemes. Moreover, the excess reddening at low impact parameters compared to the M\'{e}nard result is consistent with newer results in the literature (Section \ref{sec:literature}) and suggests active star formation in the WISExSCOS sample. 

However, the change in the amplitude of the signal with the number of \av redshift bins is somewhat puzzling to me. Not only does increasing the number of bins change the amplitude of the reddening profile, it seems to possibly change the slope as well. I am further puzzled by the different behavior of the two samples: for the high-redshift catalog, calculating \av in more bins lowers the amplitude of the signal, but for the high-density catalog, it increases it slightly. The histograms and statistics for \av presented in Section \ref{sec:results:histograms} suggest that the disappearance of the high-redshift signal with increased bins is perhaps attributable to a systematic excess in \av when it is calcuated in two redshift bins.  

The redshift binning dependence has a precedent in the first iteration of this analysis (Figure \ref{fig:old}, which also demonstrated sensitivity to the number of redshift bins in which \av is computed. As the number of redshift bins was increased, the slope of the extinction profile appeared to get steeper. A sharp change in the magnitude of the extinction at 200-400 kpc is visible in Figures \ref{fig:extinction}--\ref{fig:profile_dustmodels}; moreover, at high impact parameters the random subtraction is \textit{adding} power. I considered the relative contributions of the different terms in the $\xi_{N A_V}$ estimator defined in Equation \ref{eqn:xi_estimator}. An example extinction profile (high density redMaGiC, \av calculated in 8 redshift bins) is shown in the left panel of Figure \ref{fig:fgr_vs_not}. It is apparent that the random-foreground, real-background cross-correlation is driving the excess at $r > 5$ Mpc.  Without that subtraction (profile in teal and dark blue lines), the extinction falls off as one might expect. The right panel of \ref{fig:fgr_vs_not} shows analogous behavior for the high-redshift sample: subtracting a $R_N D_{A_V}$ component makes the signal all but vanish. 

My first thought was that my random foreground catalog algorithm must have a bug, but the sanity check of Figure \ref{fig:null_profile}~ shows similar behavior when the redMaGiC randoms are used as a random foreground catalog. This suggests that rather than my foreground random being incorrect, our $\xi_{N A_V}$ estimator may be flawed.  I believe that something more like a Landy-Szalay estimator would work (dark blue line, left panel of Figure \ref{fig:fgr_vs_not}). However, Figure \ref{fig:lrg_halo} provides a counterargument: the random-subtracted extinction profile in \textit{doesn't} display this excess, but the \textit{raw} signal does.

\section{Next steps and open questions}\label{sec:next_steps}

There are a few low-level tasks to knock out.  

\begin{itemize}
\item Measuring the extinction around GAIA stars is an easy and obvious null test that I should do again.
\item Fixing the variances on the treecorr correlations, since we know the defaults are too small. 
\item Re-examine whether the Milky Way defaults for $R_V$, $A_V$ I used in the extinction models are appropriate for our galaxy samples.
\end{itemize}

As stated above, before publishing these results, I would like to expand the analysis to more catalogs. I tried to write the code in a way that facilitates using different catalogs, and doing so heads off any criticism about relying solely on DES data. 
\begin{itemize}
\item Using the GALEX-SDSS-WISE Legacy Catalog as a foreground has the advantage of providing star formation rates, enabling an examination of extinction as a function of SFR. 
\item Using the DES MAGLIM catalog would vastly increase the number of galaxies available for reddening analysis. I could then reevaluate some of the odd trends with the high-redshift redMaGiC sample, namely, a extinction profile that disappears as $A_V$ becomes less biased.
\item The most powerful alternative catalog would be the eBOSS SDSS catalog, which has millions of galaxies, lots of blue photometry sensitive to dust extinction, and would allow us to reproduce the Lan \& Mo result in extinction rather than spectroscopic absorption. 
\end{itemize} 

Fundamentally, we are aiming to say something about the physics of dust and the larger galaxy environment. The following analyses could prove quite instructive; the first two are quite simple to implement, the others are more complex (meaning, I don't know how to do them right now!). 
\begin{enumerate}

\item Segregating galaxies by type (red vs. blue) informs models of differences in the larger-scale environments these galaxies inhabit. This is an easy test that we could carry out with the data we have. 
\item Evaluating $A_V < 50$ kpc as a function of star formation provides an even more direct test of the connection between galaxy disks and their halos. 
\item I would like to figure out a way to get a surface dust mass as a function of radius from the galaxy. Analyzing the eBOSS galaxy sample in Lan \& Mo (2018) would be an intermediate step, but it would be nice to have something more general. \label{item:a}
\item Fitting some kind of halo model to our extinction profiles would also be cool. I don't know how to implement it, but I think it would be very illuminating to connect halo mass with dust abundance and extent. \label{item:b}
\item Proving that our estimator can recover a known quantity of dust would be a really good cross-check of our methods. Analyzing the outputs of IllustrisTNG galaxies, which include dust, could be a way to do this. \label{item:c}
\end{enumerate} 

For analyses \ref{item:a} and \ref{item:b}, this might be a good opportunity to reach out to our old friend Brandon Hensley, who doubtless knows the best way to go from extinction to some sort of physical model. For analysis \ref{item:c}, Figure \ref{fig:tng} shows that this is at least possible in theory. I'm sure I can figure it out (or put a student on it!). 

Moving to open questions, I do wonder what is happening with those WISExSCOS/redMaGiC high-redshift measurements, particularly since the detection of the LRG extinction profile is unambiguous. erhaps the combination of WISExSCOS and redMaGiC does not have enough constraining power by itself: the WISE catalog is comparatively thin, with a few hundred thousand objects, while the low-redshift high-density redMaGiC catalog has 630,300 galaxies. I may try relaxing the WISExSCOS redshift cut to include more galaxies. My speculation is that the ``extinction'' profile we saw with $n_{zbins} = 2$ was actually the non-zero mean value of an $A_V$ estimated in too few redshift bins. 

The question of foreground-random real-background cross-correlation is also puzzling me. At first, I believed the profile obtained with the Landry-Szaly-style estimators in Figure \ref{fig:fgr_vs_not} more than the fully-subtracted model. The reddening that emerged at high impact parameters after foreground subtraction in the hi-$z$ foreground, low-$z$ background correlation (Figure \ref{fig:null_profile}) confirmed my suspicion that $\xi_{A_V}$ shouldn't include the $R_N\ D_{A_V}$ term. However, foreground subtraction in Figure \ref{fig:lrg_halo} actually removes the extra reddening at high impact parameter. So, now I am just puzzled. If you have insights or suggestion, I am willing to consider them! 

Before too much time passes, we should also strategize about what to put in the paper\footnote{I am in favor of publishing several small papers rather than infrequent 50-page behemoths. Though this kind of jockeying sounds venal, at my career stage, publication record is a non-negligible consideration.}! Eric mentioned UV studies, and Jim had been planning a dust emission study for a while. Are either of these still in progress? Before the break, Eric and I were exploring the decomposition of the dust model into a few orthogonal axes. I have decided to abandon that line of inquiry in favor of focusing on the results we have, or can easily obtain, and connecting those to models of circumgalactic dust. If this project has legs, an orthogonal decomposition of the extinction vector would make a cool follow-up project.

\section{Conference abstract}\label{sec:abstract}

I will be presenting a talk at ``The Physics and Impact of Astrophysical Dust: from Star Formation through Cosmology,'' an Aspen Center for Physics Winter Conference from March 3--8th. 

\textbf{Title}: 
New Evidence for Megaparsec-scale Dust Using Maximum Likelihood Estimation

\textbf{Abstract}: 
One of the more surprising astrophysical discoveries of the last decade has been an anomalous reddening of quasars consistent with dust halos (and associated large-scale structure) at cosmological scales. While this finding of extended dust halos has been confirmed several times, I will present a test of this result from a new angle: the use of maximum-likelihood formalism to construct an ``optimal estimator" for reddening of galaxy colors by dust grains. By correlating the presence of a foreground galaxy with the excess (non-cosmological) reddening of a background galaxy, I find fresh evidence for the existence of megaparsec-scale dust, transitioning from the one- to two-halo regime at scales of 100-300 kpc. While the initial analysis used the redMaGiC catalog of luminous red galaxies -- notable for its tight dispersion in color and its well-constrained photometric redshifts -- as a background sample, the result appears robust to multiple foreground-background galaxy catalog combinations. I will present an overview of our findings and outline our plans to search for sub-millimeter emission from the extended dust halos in our galaxy sample.
