\section{Motivation\label{sec:intro}}
%n This is pretty much copied and pasted from ADAP, but that's OK since it's our own words!

Most of the universe's atoms are hidden from plain sight: stars account for only 10\% of baryons, while the remaining 90\% reside in two gaseous phases that are extremely difficult to observe because of their low densities: the circumgalactic medium (CGM), filling the dark matter halos hosting galaxies, and the more extended intergalactic medium (IGM).  Despite being the focus of intense theoretical and observational research, the CGM and IGM remain poorly understood. This matters because abundance and spatial extent of dust in the Universe is a key component of galaxy formation models, comprises a spatially varying foreground screen that has practical implications for supernova cosmology, and fundamentally limits the homogeneity of samples selected for wide-field surveys. 

While attempting to measure magnification of 85,000 background quasars by 20 million normal galaxies in SDSS images, \cite{menard2010measuring} found a smooth extinction profile extending beyond 1 Mpc. A similar measurement is performed by~\cite{peek2015dust}, using as their background sources SDSS Luminous Red Galaxies (LRGs), whose remarkably small dispersion in optical colors allows for high accuracy with a smaller sample size. Together, these measurements imply that more than half of the dust in the universe lies outside galactic disks. This result is highly unexpected: the presence of dust well into the two-halo regime of the matter correlation function defies the predictions of hydrodynamic simulations of dust production and survival, under-predicting the observed measurements of~\cite{menard2010measuring, peek2015dust} by an order of magnitude~\cite{mckinnon2016dust, vogelsberger2017uniformity}. 

Beyond these measurements, investigators have searched for dust-induced extinction of background sources near a galaxy (Zaritsky 1994), or in stacks of clusters (Chelouche et al. 2007, Bovy et al. 2008). A direct detection of circumgalactic dust (CGD) at the $\ge 200$ kpc radii probed by the work of ~\cite{menard2010measuring, peek2015dust} would allow for a meaningful comparison to the simulations of cosmological dust abundance such as the ones by~\cite{mckinnon2016dust, vogelsberger2017uniformity}.

For example, supernova (SN) measurements have become a key ingredient in cosmological parameter estimation because of their constant (and large) luminosity as a function of their light curve. The apparent magnitude of a supernova is thus a proxy for its distance. However, a number of effects introduce scatter into the relationship between SN luminosity and distance, including luminosity?light curve shape dependence, a luminosity?color relation, magnification from gravitational lensing and intergalactic extinction. Modelling of SN physics and large sample sizes can average away most of this variance, but not the poorly understood reddening from cosmic dust. For SNe in particular, Ménard et al. (2010b) show that dust affects cosmological parameters such as the matter density of the universe  ?_m and the dark energy equation of state parameter w at the level of a few per cent, which is comparable to the current statistical errors. 

In this context, \cite{menard2010measuring} made an unusual discovery while attempting to measure the magnification of 85,000 background quasars by 20 million galaxies in SDSS images. They found a smooth reddening profile detectable out to 10 Mpc from the foreground galaxies; they ascribe this reddening to a circumgalactic dust halo at cosmological scales. A handful of other studies (e.g,~\cite{peek2015dust}) also find evidence for excess reddening by foreground galaxies. %of background quasar light  as a function of impact parameter from foreground galaxies. 
%Reddening out to 10 Mpc (clusters are typically 1-2 Mpc across) implies . 
These results implies that more than half of the dust in the universe lies outside galactic disks, and are in strong tension with the predictions of hydrodynamic simulations of dust processing and survival. 

If this excess dust is real, it has serious implications for all cluster cosmology surveys. All weak lensing analysis depends on accurate galaxy colors to obtain distance estimates, both for creating background galaxy samples and for fitting masses to the galaxy shear signal. A halo of dust at cosmological scales would skew the models used to turn galaxy colors into distances, and may ultimately bias cosmological parameter constraints. An extended halo would also indicate the need to revisit assumptions about how dust enters the CGM, and how long it can survive.


\section{Methods\label{sec:methods}}

A screen of dust will Rayleigh scatter incoming light, resulting in an attenuation or extinction and changing its spectrum by reddening it. The total extinction $\rm A_\lambda$ quantifies wavelength-dependent dust absorption; reddening is usually parametrized by the selective extinction red versus blue light: $\rm  E(B-V) = (B-V)_{\rm meas} - (B-V)_0$. %(or similarly, $\rm E(g-i) = (g-i)_{\rm meas} - (g-i)_0 $ for SDSS filters). 
%More dust yields a more positive selective extinction $\rm E(B-V)$. 
The steepness of $\rm A_\lambda$ as a function of wavelength is usually parametrized by 
\begin{equation}
R_V = \rm{A_V / E(B - V)}
\end{equation}
For interstellar Milky Way dust, $R_V \sim 3.1$; dense molecular clouds have $R_V \sim 5$. 

% Somewhat wishy-washy, tighten up during editing
These statements are generally true for dust-induced reddening and extinction. However, we are looking for a faint, extended signal, buried within the noise and color scatter of our background sample. Magnitude differences like $\rm E(B - V)$ are instructive, but somewhat simplistic given the subtlety of the effect. Here, we use maximum likelihood parameter estimation to find a linear combination of wavelengths that represents the best chance of finding the extinction. This optimal estimator is applied to a sample of background galaxies that have been binned in redshift space to control for cosmological (not dust-induced) reddening. Finally, the resulting dust extinction measure is correlated with the presence of foreground galaxies to search for extended, anomalous reddening consistent with dust halos at cosmological scales. We describe our approach in greater detail below, noting that for the sake of readability all vector or matrix quantities appear in boldface. 

\subsection{A Maximum Likelihood Estimator for Dust Extinction}\label{ssec:estimator}

% intro wording should be refined... words about p{eq:likelihood}arameter estimation?
For a uniform prior probability distribution of a given parameter, the log-likelihood of XXX a given value is XXX:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
\log L = \rm \left [\mathbf{D} - \mathbf{M}(\rho)\right ] \frac{\mathbf{C}^{-1}}{2} \left[\mathbf{D} - \mathbf{M}(\rho)\right ]^{\rm T}
\end{equation}

For galaxies, the data vector $\mathbf{D}$ is an array of magnitudes, e.g.,

\begin{equation}\label{eq:data_vector}
\mathbf{\rm D} = \{\rm u,g,r,i,z,W1,W2,...\}
\end{equation}
while the covariance of the data $\mathbf{C}$ encapsulates both the magnitude measurement uncertainties and cosmic variance. The term $\mathbf{M}(\rho)$ is the best-fit model describing the data $\mathbf{D}$; $\rho$ is the unknown parameter for which Equation~\ref{eq:likelihood} is solved -- here, a unit $\rm A_V$. Taylor expansion of $\mathbf{M}(\rho)$ yields a simple linear approximation for the effect of $\rho$:
\begin{equation}\label{model}
\rm \mathbf{M} = M_0 + \rho\ \dMdp
\end{equation}
 
 In this expansion, $M_0$ are the intrinsic colors of galaxies in the background galaxy sample, without any reddening from dust. The $\rho$ \dMinline~ term is the response of galaxies to a unit dust; \dMinline~is a fiducial (theoretical) extinction curve for dust. To replicate the work of~\cite{menard2010measuring}, we use the extinction model of~\cite{fitzpatrick1999correcting} for $R_V = 3.1$ Milky Way-type dust. 

 The optimal estimator $\hat{\rho}$ for the dust extinction $\rho$, such that $\rho$ maximizes the likelihood in Equation~\ref{eq:likelihood}, is given as: 

\begin{equation}
\hat{\rho} = \rm \left[ \frac{ \dMdp \ \mathbf{C}^{-1} \left (\mathbf{D} - M_0 \right )} {\dMdp\ \mathbf{C}^{-1 }\ \dMdp} \right ] \label{eq:estimator}
\end{equation}

where by the Cram\'{e}r-Rao bound, the uncertainty on $\hat{\rho}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
\sigma^2_{\hat{\rho}} = \rm \dMdp\ \mathbf{C}^{-1 }\ \dMdp \label{eq:RCO}
\end{equation}
  %\begin{comment}
 The argument leading to Equations~\ref{eq:estimator} and~\ref{eq:RCO} can also be presented as follows. Each galaxy in the (background) sample has a set of colors
 \begin{equation}\label{commentEqn:model}
\mathbf{D} = M_0+ {\rm A_V}\ dM + \text{noise}
 \end{equation}
 The noise term is independent of $\rm A_V$, and behaves as a multivariate Gaussian with a covariance $\mathbf{C}$. In the absence of dust, $\mathbf{D}$ minus the mean color of the sample $M_0$ is simply a set of values points drawn from the distribution described by $\mathbf{C}$. The expression 
 \begin{equation}\label{commentEqn:significance}
 \frac{\mathbf{C}^{-1}}{2} \left[\mathbf{D} - \mathbf{M}(\rho)\right ]^{\rm T}
\end{equation} 
parametrizes how significant the deviation in $\mathbf{D}$ is from $\rm {M_0}$. We may then ask how much of the observed deviation is in the direction we would expect from dust by projecting Equation~\ref{commentEqn:significance} into the theoretical dust curve \dMinline:
  \begin{equation}
  \left (\mathbf{D} - M_0 \right )\ \mathbf{C}^{-1} \ \dMdp 
 \end{equation}
Rescaling (calibrating) this deviation in background galaxy color from the total dust column, relative to the reddening from a ``unit dust'' column, yields the final form of the estimator in Equations~\ref{eq:estimator}
\begin{equation}
\hat{\rho} = \rm \left[ \frac{ \dMdp \ \mathbf{C}^{-1} \left (\mathbf{D} - M_0 \right )} {\dMdp\ \mathbf{C}^{-1 }\ \dMdp} \right ]
\end{equation}
The factor
\begin{equation}
\frac{\rm \partial M / \partial \rho} {\dMdp\ \mathbf{C}^{-1 }\ \dMdp}
\end{equation}
is then a normalized reddening vector per ``unit dust''

 % I'm not entirely sure that this is right  -- Eric's help will be awesome here eventually
Equations~\ref{model} to~\ref{eq:RCO} implicitly assume that the theoretical extinction curve \dMinline~accurately describes the CGD. For consistency with~\cite{menard2010measure}, we take a fiducial value of \dMinline~that corresponds to $R_V = 3.1$ dust. However, this need not be the case -- circumgalactic dust may have a different reddening vector. To test this idea, we apply the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization procedure to the fiducial \dMinline. The resulting set of orthonormal vectors span the ``extinction basis,'' into which the true CGD reddening vector is projected. 

%\begin{comment}
For observations made in the $griz$ filter set, and taking the model of~\cite{fitzpatrick1999correcting} for $R_V = 3.1$ dust, the fiducial \dMinline~extinction vector is
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{v_{\rm fid}} &= \left[ 1.12224688, 0.82747095, 0.62680647, 0.47880753 \right ] \label{eq:fid}\\
&\equiv {\rm A_V}^{\rm fid}
\end{align*}
The orthonormal basis vector $\mathbf{v_1}^{\rm ON}$ is constructed as 
\begin{gather}
    \mathbf{v_1} = \left [ 1, 0, 0, 0 \right ] \\
    \mathbf{v'_1} = \mathbf{v_1} - \langle \mathbf{v_1} | \mathbf{v_{\rm fid}}\rangle \ast \mathbf{v}_{\rm fid}\\
     \mathbf{v_1}^{\rm ON} =  \frac{\mathbf{v'_1}} { \left\| \mathbf{v'}_1 \right\| }\label{eq:ON}
\end{gather}
%\end{comment}
The procedure is then repeated for $\mathbf{v_2} = \left [ 0, 1, 0, 0 \right ]$ and $\mathbf{v_2}=\left [ 0, 0, 1, 0 \right ]$, taking care to subtract the projection of the previous vectors onto each new vector. 
% Following may be easier 
The true CGD extinction can then be reconstructed from the individual magnitudes %of the reddening signal along each basis vector 
through a simple $\chi^2$ minimization, i.e.,

\begin{equation}
\chi^2 = \sum \frac{\left ( k_{\rm fid} - \alpha_i \  k_i \right )^2}{\left ( \sigma^2_{\rm fid} + \alpha^2_i \ \sigma^2_i \right )}, i = 1,2...
\end{equation} 

where $k$ is the extinction signal measured measured along a given basis vector: either the fiducial \dMinline~($k_{\rm fid}$) or one of the orthogonal components ($k_1, k_2, ...$) and $\sigma$ is the uncertainty of the extinction measurement. The true CGD extinction is thus
\begin{equation}
k^{\rm true} = k^{\rm fid} + \alpha_1 \ k_1 + \alpha_2 \ k_2 + \alpha_3 \ k_3 + ...
\end{equation}


This orthogonalization procedure is analogous to a principal component analysis, where each basis vector captures some of the variance of the reddened galaxy colors. Positive or negative value in the individual components in~\ref{eq:ON} mean that the ``principal component" $\mathbf{v}_{\rm fid}$ either under or overfits the actual trend at that radius. 

\subsection{Galaxy-Reddening Correlation Function\label{ssec:treecorr}}

%There's some repetition here, also the wording is garbage. Does it even need to be here?
With the dust extinction estimator above in~\ref{ssec:estimator}, we may then correlate the magnitude of dust-induced extinction with the presence of a foreground galaxy. We do this with the \texttt{TreeCorr}\footnote{https://github.com/rmjarvis/TreeCorr} package~\cite{jarvis2004skewness}, specifically the galaxy-kappa cross-correlation. Though intended to correlate galaxy counts with shear signal (i.e., $\kappa$) the code is flexible enough to accommodate any scalar quantity. Here, we correlate foreground galaxy counts with the extinction of background galaxies as determined with the estimator in Equation~\ref{eq:estimator} for the fiducial dust model and its orthogonal components derived in Equation~\ref{eq:ON}. A variety of other two-point correlations, such as foreground star/background galaxy extinction, are performed as null tests. Foreground and background galaxy-galaxy two-point correlation functions are also computed. All correlations are taken in twenty impact parameter bins, from 0.2 to 200 arcminutes (or 200 kiloparsecs to 10 megaparsecs in the average foreground galaxy rest frame). This range of impact parameters matches the physical scales probed in~\cite{menard2010measuring}. % in the average rest frame of our foreground galaxy samples.

Our background catalog for extinction measurements consists of 599,554 luminous red galaxies drawn from the Dark Energy Survey (DES, citation needed?) Year 1 data release. Galaxies are selected with the red sequence Matched-filter Galaxy Catalog (redMaGiC) algorithm~\cite{rozo2016redmagic}), which fits every galaxy with a red sequence template (itself derived from the redMaPPer red-sequence cluster finder~\cite{rykoff2014red}), computes the resulting photometric redshift and luminosity, and retains only those galaxies for which the galaxy luminosity is high and the fit is good ($\chi^2 < \chi^2_\text{max}$). Rather than a hard cutoff, the redMaGiC selection threshold $\chi^2_\text{max}$ is allowed to float with redshift to assure a constant co-moving galaxy density. Photometric redshifts of redMaGiC galaxies have less than 1\% bias relative to spectroscopic redshifts over the entire range of the catalog ($0.15 < z < 0.85$).
