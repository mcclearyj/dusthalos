\section{Motivation\label{sec:intro}}
%n This is pretty much copied and pasted from ADAP, but that's OK since it's our own words!
most of the universe?s atoms are hidden from plain sight: stars account for only 10\% of baryons, while the remaining 90\% reside in two gaseous phases that are extremely difficult to observe because of their low densities: the circumgalactic medium (CGM), filling the dark matter halos hosting galaxies, and the more extended intergalactic medium (IGM).  Despite being the focus of intense theoretical and observational research, the CGM and IGM remain poorly understood. This matters because abundance and spatial extent of dust in the Universe is a key component of galaxy formation models, comprises a spatially varying foreground screen that has practical implications for supernova cosmology, and fundamentally limits the homogeneity of samples selected for wide-field surveys. 

While attempting to measure magnification of 85,000 background quasars by 20 million normal galaxies in SDSS images, Ménard et al. (2010) found a smooth extinction profile extending beyond 1 Mpc. Peek et al. (2014) perform a similar measurement using as their background sources SDSS Luminous Red Galaxies (LRGs), whose remarkably small dispersion in optical colors allows for high accuracy with a smaller sample size. Together, these measurements imply that more than half of the dust in the universe lies outside galactic disks. This result is highly unexpected: the presence of dust well into the two-halo regime of the matter correlation function defies the predictions of hydrodynamic simulations of dust production and survival, under-predicting the observed Ménard and Peek measurements by an order of magnitude (McKinnon et al. 2017). 

Beyond the Peek and Menard measurements, investigators have searched for dust-induced extinction of background sources near a galaxy (Zaritsky 1994), or in stacks of clusters (Chelouche et al. 2007, Bovy et al. 2008). A direct detection of circumgalactic dust at the $\ge 200$ kpc radii probed by the work of Ménard et al. (2010a) and Peek et al. (2014) would allow for a meaningful comparison to the simulations of cosmological dust abundance such as the ones by McKinnon et al. (2017) and Vogelsberger et al. (2018).

A given extinction profile is compatible with a wide range of dust masses and grain sizes, and has little constraining power by itself.  When combined with emission profiles of dust, the degeneracies are broken and a dust mass column density, the dust temperature and the dust emissivity averaged along the line-of-sight are obtained. These pieces of information allow for an evaluation of dust formation and processing paradigms.
