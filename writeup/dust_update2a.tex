\section{Executive Summary}\label{execsumm}

This is a follow-up to the comprehensive ``Circumgalactic dust halos update'' document circulated in October 2023. Here are the principal updates since that time: 
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Implementation of major modeling updates and code improvements ($\S$\ref{sec:code}), including: 
		\begin{enumerate}
		\item Redshift-based matching for augmenting redMaGiC random catalogs with galaxy colors.% (Figure \ref{fig:redshiftmatch}). 
		\item Bug fix in the generation of random (foreground) catalogs; my previous algorithm, which randomly sampled HEALPixels and then calculated the central RA and Dec of said HEALPixel, did not uniformly cover the survey area. The change led to nicer-looking results, especially for the high-z redMaGiC catalog. 
		\item Patch-based variance estimation in the correlation function calculations, as strongly recommended in TreeCorr documentation.

\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}
Since my last update over a year ago, several exciting and highly germane papers have been published. Results include an interesting hydrosim study of dust survival in the IGM, a new model for dust extinction, and an interesting new update to the canonical SFD98 extinction map. 
\subsection{Gordon et al. 2023}
My previous results were based on dust models published around 2000, like Calzetti et al.~2000 and Fitzpatrick 1999. When I presented these results at the Aspen Center for Physics workshop, Daniella Calzetti told me that she was flattered that I was using her extinction law but that it was out of date. She pointed me to an updated model published by Karl Gordon et al.~2023 (G23, \cite{2023ApJ...950...86G}). Using four independent spectroscopically measured dust extinction curves and dozens of individual sightlines, G23 presents a single $R_V$-dependent extinction relation from 912 \AA~to 32 microns. In their words, the main advance of their approach is that ``all previous R(V) relationships were based on a combination of spectroscopic and photometric measurements and/or did not cover the full wavelength range.'' Their principal result, an updated $A_\lambda/A_V$ relation, is shown in Figure 8 of G23. 
They obtain this relation by first fitting a linear $A_\lambda/A_V$ at each wavelength; polynomials are then fit to the resulting coefficients piecewise in five wavelength regimes (FUV, NUV, optical, near- and mid-IR); the polynomials are smoothed at the boundaries to obtain a continuous function (see Equation 5 of that paper).They find that the characteristic FUV rise and $2175 {\rm \AA}$ bump are dependent on $R_V$, but that 10 and 20 micron features are not. The largest offsets between their extinction laws and other, comparable studies are in the FUV and the NIR (cf. Figure 8, lower panels). Interestingly, their extinction relation lies in the middle of the older studies' extinction relations. In other words, the G23 result appears to be a ``golden mean."

\subsection{CSFD}
I saw Yun-Ki Chiang give a talk on this topic at the Aspen dust workshop, and then my COSMOS-Web colleague Nao Suzuki called my attention to this result again at the COSMOS 2024 meeting in Tokyo. The basic premise of the paper is that the classic Schlegel, Finkbeiner, \& Davis (1998, SFD) dust map has significant contamination from the cosmic infrared background, that is, the two-halo term of large-scale structure. This fact has been discovered and rediscovered in the literature \cite{10.1093/pasj/65.2.43, 10.1093/pasj/59.1.205}, including by Chiang and Brice M{\'e}nard themselves \cite{2019ApJ...870..120C} and our old friend Daniel Lenz \cite{2017ApJ...846...38L}.
The \cite{10.1093/pasj/65.2.43} finding is particularly interesting to me, as they show that the surface number density of SDSS galaxies increases with the value of the E(B-V) selective extinction in SFD. This is the opposite of the effect one would expect in areas of high extinction but is consistent with LSS contamination in the SFD map. 

To remove the traces of LSS from the SFD map, Chiang first creates a tomographic map of the LSS using generic tracers like quasars or galaxies, computes the angular cross-correlation these with $E(B-V)$ to produce an LSS extinction map $I_{LSS}(\phi)$ and then reconstructs this $I_{LSS}(\phi)$ map with a large number of empirical templates $T_{LSS, i}(\phi)$. The $T_{LSS, i}(\phi)$ are ``sky maps of density contrast built from generic extragalactic tracers'' like quasars or galaxies, and their combination, or rather the reconstructed $I_{LSS}(\phi)$, is then subtracted from the canonical SFD map to produce a new map cleaned of LSS contamination (so-called CSFD). Full details of the production of CSFD may be found in Chiang's 2023 paper \cite{2023ApJ...958..118C}; an example SFD-CSFD comparison is shown in Figure \ref{fig:csfd}.  


\subsection{Dust as a supernova systematic}

As we all know, intergalactic dust introduces significant scatter to Type Ia supernova (SN Ia) distance estimates. According to \cite{2023arXiv231112098R}, dust is the second-largest source of uncertainty in SNe Type Ia cosmological parameter constraints, accounting for 11.5\% of the total variance. Current SNe analyses include intergalactic dust reddening as a function of wavelength and SN redshift in the systematic error budget, but because the magnitude of the effect is somewhat uncertain, do not correct the distance measures themselves. My COSMOS-Web colleague, Nao Suzuki, pointed out that if we can differentiate extinction laws according to foreground galaxy properties, as we appear to do, then we could derive a more precise calibration for foreground reddening of supernovae. So, our work could have quite high impact.

The redMaGiC catalogs (both real and random) have positions and redshifts, but not magnitudes. To obtain 
colors for the real catalogs, I join them with their counterparts in the Y3 GOLD catalog on the 
\texttt{COADD\_OBJECT\_ID} parameter. To do the same for the random galaxy catalogs, these objects were originally assigned photometry from the Y3 GOLD catalog randomly, with no regard for redshift. Concerned that this might introduce systematic error, I implemented redshift-based matching of random galaxies. The algorithm is fairly simple:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Generate some number of redshift bins (I chose seventeen) and digitize the photometry and random-galaxy catalogs accordingly. 
\item For each redshift bin, generate an array of random integers with as many entries as there are random galaxies in this bin and with an upper limit of the number of photometry galaxies in this bin. 
\item Assign the $g$, $r$, $i$, $z$ magnitudes of galaxies in the photometry catalog to the corresponding entries in the random-galaxy catalog elementwise.
\end{enumerate}

The result is shown in Figure \ref{fig:redshiftmatch}. Aside from the last two bins of the $i-z$ color space for the high-redshift sample, the match appears pretty good.  Note that the photometry catalogs used for this procedure are the color-augmented redMaGiC galaxy catalogs, not the full Y3 GOLD catalog. Using the full Y3 GOLD catalog produced a mismatch in colors, which is attributable to redMaGiC galaxies occupying a rather unique color space. 

It should be noted that adopting color-matched randoms change did not visibly affect extinction profiles.

\subsubsection{Random catalog generation overhaul}\label{sec:code:randoms}

As I described in the first update, for cases where random catalogs are not available, I developed a class called FgRandom (defined in \texttt{fg\_random.py}). In the first release, FgRandom used a seeded random number generator and the survey mask's HEALPix \texttt{NSIDE} parameter to randomly select HEALPixels and converting those to RA, Dec using the HEALPy library's \texttt{pix2ang()} function. An example of a random catalog generated with the Fall 2023 version of FgRandom is shown in the top panel of Figure \ref{fig:fgrand}. I originally stated that the moir\'{e} effect was an artifact of projection and not present in the catalog itself. However, it never quite sat right with me, and my original statement turned out \textit{not} to be true! It should have been obvious earlier, but a point selected in this way will always sit in the center of the HEALPixel. For large NSIDE values, the final layout will still look OK, since each cell is quite small. For small NSIDE values, where each cell is large, the layout of points will be decidedly non-random.

I now build random catalogs with a more boring but less buggy method: sample a large number of points uniformly in $(\alpha,\, \sin\delta)$ (not uniformly in $\alpha, \delta$!), then apply the supplied HEALPixel mask. For very large galaxy catalogs like SDSS, generating an equivalent random catalog with almost a billion objects in one go is too much for my high-performance computing cluster to handle, at least with my sloppy astronomer code. \subsubsection{TreeCorr variance upgrade}

%The TreeCorr package is the backbone of our analysis, so implementing it correctly is crucial if we are to trust our results. 
Error bars on \av profiles in update \#1 represented the default \texttt{TreeCorr} shot-noise estimate of the covariance. For \texttt{NKCorrelation} (our main correlator), this includes Poisson statistics of counting (N) and the ``point variance'' of scalar quantities (K). 
% this is pretty close to TreeCorr copypasta. 
However, this value underestimates the true uncertainty: the default TreeCorr covariance does not capture \textit{sample} variance, the variation of the signal across the survey area. Its contribution should not be ignored; per the TreeCorr documentation, sample variance generally dominates the total variance at large spatial scales. Sample variance can be estimated by partitioning the survey area into contiguous patches, performing the measurement separately in each, and then quantifying the variation in the results across the patches.

TreeCorr offers access to this calculation through its \texttt{sample}, \texttt{bootstrap}, and \texttt{marked\_bootstrap} methods for covariance estimation.%, which can be specified in the \texttt{NKCorrelation} constructor through the \texttt{var\_method} parameter. 
All of these estimation methods are now accessible in our code via the \texttt{treecorr\_params} block of the \texttt{dust\_calc\_runner.py} configuration file, i.e., \texttt{dust\_calc\_config.yaml}.\footnote{The \texttt{bootstrap} and \texttt{marked\_bootstrap} methods also take a \texttt{num\_bootstrap} parameter that sets the number of bootstrap estimates.} The number of patches into which to divide the survey area is set through \texttt{treecorr\_npatch} in the \texttt{background\_catalog} block of the \texttt{dust\_calc\_runner.py} config, which is passed to the ``signal'' (real background galaxy catalog) \texttt{treecorr.Catalog()} constructor. Since consistency in sample variance estimates across all terms in the reddening estimator requires that the sky patch centers be the same, patch centers for the foreground and random catalogs' \texttt{treecorr.Catalog()} instances are set to the values in the signal \texttt{treecorr.Catalog()}.

All results presented in this update use the \texttt{sample} covariance estimation method.

\subsection{Model improvements}\label{sec:modelupdates}

\subsubsection{Reddening calculation: G23 extinction and weighted average color}
The G23 extinction model is available through the \texttt{dust\_extinction} software package \cite{Gordon2024}.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/karllark/dust_extinction}}. Implementing it in the code was very simple and amounted to adding an import statement for this newer \texttt{dust\_extinction} package in the \texttt{src/extinction\_model.py}. Both G23 and the older CCM89 models are available in \texttt{dust\_extinction}; the \texttt{extinction} Python package is still imported in case someone wants to see results with other old models: calzetti00, odonnell94, fitz99. As before, the default $R_V$ is 3.1 but any value can be specified in the \texttt{dust\_calc\_runner.py} config. 

Additionally, in the \av calculation of \texttt{reddening\_calc.py}, the mean galaxy color in each redshift bin is now computed with a \textit{weighted} average, not a straight arithmetic mean as was done before. De-meaning the galaxy colors before calculation of our \av is an essential part of the analysis, and I thought that adopting a weighted average would make a bigger difference than it did. However, the effect on the results was minor, probably because redMaGiC galaxies are all big and bright and occupy a tight color locus, so the unweighted mean color is a good guess for the weighted mean color. The new code is reproduced below: 

\small
\begin{verbatim}
# Stack the extracted magnitudes and uncertainties into 2D arrays
data = np.vstack(data_list)
errors = np.vstack(error_list)

# Compute the average in each band, weighting by the inverse
# of the square of the uncertainties
weights = 1 / errors**2
weighted_avg = np.average(data, weights=weights, axis=1)

# De-mean the galaxy magnitudes in each redshift bin
colors = (data.T - weighted_avg).T

\end{verbatim}
\normalsize

\subsubsection{CSFD}

As mentioned earlier, Nao pointed me to this CSFD paper \cite{2023ApJ...958..118C}. Since the amplitude of the $E(B-V)$ residual was non-negligible ($10^{-4}$), the update to CSFD seemed worth implementing. I located a nice software package called \texttt{dustmapper}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/gregreen/dustmaps}} to query the CSFD dust map and obtain extinction coefficients for a given set of coordinates. Specifically, there is a handy \texttt{dustmaps.csfd.CSFDQuery} method that takes as input an RA, Dec list in SkyCoords format and returns an array of E(B-V) values. An $R_V$ value is used to convert those into extinction coefficients and then used to de-redden SDSS and DES magnitudes. 
%Using SFD maps to obtain E(B-V), then apply band-specific extinction coefficients to get extinction values.
Subtracting these extinction values A$_{\rm X,\, CSFD}$ from the observed magnitudes \texttt{MAG\_X}, we may obtain the dereddened magnitudes:
\small
\begin{verbatim}
MAG_X_CORRECTED_CSFD = MAG_X - A_X_CSFD
\end{verbatim}
\normalsize

For the DES magnitudes, I adapted the magnitude prescription of Pandey et al.~2021, replacing the previous $A_{X,\mathrm{SFD98}}$ with the equivalent CSFD value: 
\small
\begin{verbatim}
MAG_X_CORRECTED_CSFD = MOF_CM_MAG + DELTA_MAG_Y4 + DELTA_MAG_CHROM - A_X_CSFD
\end{verbatim}
\normalsize

Note that to calculate extinction values correctly, the per-magnitude \av values were multiplied by a factor of 0.78, as described in the Sevilla-Noarbe et al.~2021 \cite{2021ApJS..254...24S}, and the effective wavelengths of each bandpass were rescaled as prescribed in Table 6 of Schlafly \& Finkbeiner 2011 \cite{2011ApJ...737..103S}. These magnitude corrections are implemented in \texttt{src/deredden\_magnitudes.py} and \\
\texttt{src/deredden\_sdss\_magnitudes.py}; the gist of the algorithm is shown in Appendix \ref{appx:deredden}. For a sanity check, I compared the \texttt{a\_sed\_sfd98\_g} $g$-band extinction value provided in the Y3 GOLD catalog with the value obtained with \texttt{dustmaps.SFD} for one of the redMaGiC galaxy samples. The difference between the two was reassuringly small ($-1.86\ee{-9}$). 

As one might expect, the switch to CSFD had a notable effect on the amplitude of the signal and varied by sample type. For the WISExSuperCOSMOS foreground, CSFD had the effect of decreasing signal amplitude at large impact parameters. For the redMaGiC LRG sample, it had the effect of increasing the extinction at high impact parameters. Details can be found in $\S$\ref{sec:csfdvsnot}.

\section{Catalogs: Round 2}\label{sec:catalogs}

\subsection{Southern sky: redMaGiC \& WISExSCOS}
All results presented in update \#1 were made with the WISExSuperCOSMOS (aka WISExSCOS) catalog as a foreground and the Dark Energy Survey redMaGiC catalogs \cite{2016MNRAS.461.1431R} as backgrounds. Since that time, there have been a couple of minor changes to the way these catalogs are implemented in our analysis. First, the redshift cut for the WISExSCOS catalog is now $z < 0.2$ from $z < 0.15$, and I have implemented an additional cut of $17 < bCalCorr < 21$ to minimize stellar confusion at the faint end. Second, a cut of $z > 0.5$ is placed on the high-density redMaGiC sample to ensure adequate foreground-background separation (the full redshift range is $0.15 < z < 0.65$). \subsection{Star catalogs for systematics testing}
Beyond new galaxy catalogs, since update \#1 I have also constructed star catalogs to test for systematic errors in our analysis. The rationale for using star catalogs is thus: unless embedded in a star-forming region, stars do not have extended halos of dust---certainly not at megaparsec scales! Similarly, stars embedded in the Milky Way should not display dust-induced color gradients as a function of projected distance from galaxies outside the Milky Way. So, using star catalogs as both foreground and background samples in the extinction calculation offers a valuable null test of our pipeline. My intuition is that a stellar foreground catalog helps validate the analysis as a whole, while a stellar background catalog with the same magnitudes as the background sample (i.e., redMaGiC) helps to validate the reddening estimator, or at least the pipeline.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

Globally, \av profiles generated in this work SFD and CSFD results agrees within one sigma with the \textit{exponent} of the M10 (\cite{2010MNRAS.406.1815M}) power law (Equation 13 in that reference), reproduced below:

\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(4.4 \pm 1.1\right) \times 10^{-3} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, \mathrm{kpc}} \right)^{-0.86 \pm 0.19}\label{eqn:menard}
\end{equation}


This match was not guaranteed and is certainly cool to see. However, we also have a couple of new and surprising results: 

\begin{enumerate}
\item For all foreground samples considered save the redMaGiC LRG, the \av at $r < 50$ \hinv kpc actually follows a significantly steeper power law than the rest of the profiles. This broken power law result has been consistent since our first experiments in 2018, so it is no fluke! 
\item The WISExSuperCOSMOS extinction profiles taper/roll off after about 3 \hinv Mpc, consistent with an earlier literature result cited in update \#1.
\item Although the exponents are roughly the same as M10, the \textit{amplitude} of our \av profiles is 2-3 times higher than the M10 result. 
\end{enumerate}

Comments for the individual samples follow.

\subsubsection{WISExSuperCOSMOS foreground}

The extinction profiles of WISExSuperCOSMOS galaxies as measured with redMaGiC background galaxies are shown in Figure \ref{fig:redmagicfits}: the result from cross-correlation with the $z > 0.5$ high-density redMaGiC sample is shown in dark blue, and the result from cross-correlation with the high-redshift redMaGiC sample is shown in light blue. Both extinction profiles follow a broken power law, with a rapid decrease from $A_V \sim 0.3$ to $A_V \sim 0.02$ in the inner 50 \hinv kpc, then a slower decline to $A_V \sim 0.02$ at 1 \hinv Mpc . At the transition to the 2-halo regime ($r \gtrsim 2$ \hinv Mpc), the extinction profiles rapidly diminish to below our detection threshold ($A_V \sim 10^{-5}$). 

I fit power laws to the WISExSuperCOSMOS extinction profiles in the 1-halo regime (50 \hinv kpc $ < r < 1$ \hinv Mpc); the inner limit is set to the point where the redMaGiC LRG and WISExSuperCOSMOS extinction profiles intersect, and the outer limit to the points where the extinction begins to roll off. 

The best-fit power law to the extinction profile obtained with the $z > 0.5$ high-density redMaGiC sample is given by Equation \ref{eqn:hidens}: 
% Equation 1
\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(1.14 \pm 0.08\right) \times 10^{-2} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, \mathrm{kpc}} \right)^{-0.84 \pm 0.04}\label{eqn:hidens}
\end{equation}

Similarly, for the extinction profile obtained with the $z > 0.5$ high-lum high-$z$ redMaGiC sample is given by Equation \ref{eqn:hiz}:
% Equation 2
\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(1.02 \pm 0.11\right) \times 10^{-2} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, \mathrm{kpc}} \right)^{-0.72 \pm 0.06}\label{eqn:hiz}
\end{equation}

Combining the two WISExSuperCOSMOS $\times$ redMaGiC profiles, the best fit power law is Equation \ref{eqn:stacked}; this is the result I plan to publish.  
% Equation 3
\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(1.08 \pm 0.07\right) \times 10^{-2} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, {\rm kpc}} \right)^{-0.77 \pm 0.04}\label{eqn:stacked}
\end{equation}



\subsubsection{redMaGiC LRG}

Cross-correlation of the redMaGiC LRG galaxy sample ($z < 0.45$ high-density redMaGiC) with the redMaGiC high-lum high-$z$ sample produced the extinction curve shown in Figure \ref{fig:redmagicfits}. As might be expected for this foreground population, there is little apparent extinction at the lowest impact parameters ($r < 50$ \hinv kpc). While qualitatively similar to the SuperCOMSOS-based results in the one-halo regime, the LRG sample shows significant extinction well into the two-halo regime. The best-fit power law to this profile is given by Equation \ref{eqn:lrg}:

\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(0.82 \pm 0.19\right) \times 10^{-2} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, \mathrm{kpc}} \right)^{-0.69 \pm 0.06}\label{eqn:lrg}
\end{equation}

The exponent and amplitude of Equation \ref{eqn:lrg} agree with the combined redMaGiC fit of Equation \ref{eqn:stacked} to within about 1 $\sigma$ through the one-halo regime ($r \lesssim 1$ Mpc).  

\subsubsection{Comparison to SFD}\label{sec:csfdvsnot}

Given the acknowledged problems of SFD, the CSFD formulation is the default Milky Way extinction correction used in this work. It may nevertheless be of interest to compare the profiles obtained with the SFD and CSFD prescriptions; this is shown for the three redMaGiC-based samples in Figure \ref{fig:csfdvsnot}. I noticed a couple of interesting trends here. First, using the SFD extinction correction didn't seem to produce much of an effect for the high-density background catalog but noticeably decreased the amplitude of the high-lum high-z profile at high impact parameters. Put differently, it appears that the SFD prescription added too much extinction at high impact parameters, and with CSFD the two redMaGiC catalogs produce consistent results for WISExSuperCOSMOS. On the other hand, compared to the SFD prescription, the CSFD prescription added a significant amount of dust at higher impact parameters for the redMaGIC LRG sample, leading to significant reddening past 1 \hinv Mpc. Although quite different from the effect for the WISExSuperCOSMOS result, this behavior for LRG is consistent with the idea that SFD overcorrected foreground extinction due to contamination from the LSS. If one assumes that the LRG is a particularly biased tracer of the LSS, then it makes sense that the CSFD prescription adds some reddening back in.

\end{equation}

The best-fit power law to the extinction profile obtained with the high-lum high-$z$ redMaGiC sample is given by Equation \ref{eqn:hizsfd}:

% Equation 3

% Equation 4
\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(6.9 \pm 2.1\right) \times 10^{-3} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, \mathrm{kpc}} \right)^{-0.87 \pm 0.14}\label{eqn:lrgsfd}
\end{equation}

For this last profile, my WLS algorithm just would not produce a reasonable fit, which forced me to adopt a conventional least-squares algorithm for the LRG fit. The prefactor of Equation \ref{eqn:lrgsfd} matches the fit of Equation \ref{eqn:lrg} within error bars, however, the exponents are in a 3 $\sigma$ disagreement. Meanwhile, the fits of Equations \ref{eqn:hidenssfd}--\ref{eqn:stackedsfd} agree with \ref{eqn:hidens}--\ref{eqn:stacked} within 1 $\sigma$.

However, that dip in the middle is somewhat mysterious to me. Its exact location also seems to depend heavily on the NSIDE of the HEALPix mask I create for the catalog. This may indicate that the mask I create is incomplete or wrong; regardless, it's I don't 100\% trust the result. For the sake of completeness, I decided to fit a power law to the outermost six points of this GSWLC \x SDSS extinction profile, with the result shown in Equation \ref{eqn:gswlc}. As one might expect from Figure \ref{fig:uglycolor}, this relationship agrees very nicely with Equations \ref{eqn:stacked} and \ref{eqn:lrg}.

\begin{equation}
A_V(r) = \left(1.5 \pm 0.4\right) \times 10^{-2} \left( \frac{r}{h^{-1}\, \mathrm{kpc}} \right)^{-0.76 \pm 0.07}\label{eqn:gswlc}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Quiescent vs. star-forming dust profiles}. Around May of 2024, I experimented with calculating extinction profiles on GSWLC-X2 data segregated by specific star formation rates (sSFR). I was very curious to see the difference in \av between quiescent galaxies, defined by the GSWLC group as having $\log_{10}\mathrm{sSFR} < -11.5 $ in the M or D GSWLC UV sub-sample, and star-forming galaxies with $\log_{10}\mathrm{sSFR} > -11.5$ in any GSWLC UV sub-sample.\footnote{https://salims.pages.iu.edu/gswlc/} With a cut at $z < 0.2$, there were about 187,000 quiescent galaxies and 208,000 actively star-forming galaxies (Figure \ref{fig:gswlcMstarHist}). The results are shown in Figure \ref{fig:gswlcMstarHist} of Appendix \ref{appx:gswlc}. While cool, Figure \ref{fig:gswlcMstarHist} left me a little bemused, particularly the extra-large dip in the middle of the profiles. Moreover, the selection functions and sky coverage are also quite heterogenous between the quiescent and star-forming samples, which surely complicates the interpretation of the result. As an additional caveat, these results are based on the out-of-date SFD extinction correction, as the CSFD Milky Way extinction correction scheme had not yet been implemented in the code. Since I wasn't even sure I understood the whole GSWLC sample result, redoing this complicated quiescent versus star-forming analysis with CSFD didn't seem worth it. Nevertheless, this is an interesting line of inquiry, so we can pick it up again after submission.

\section{Discussion and Next Steps}\label{sec:discussion}
\subsection{Comment on results}

As discussed above, the major takeaway is the consistency of our \av profile slopes with M10 across all samples but the significantly larger-than-predicted amplitude. The fact that CSFD-based profiles have slighly higher \av values than SFD-based profiles is consistent with the claim of \cite{2023ApJ...958..118C} that ``when SFD is used to derive absolute photometry for sources, Galactic extinction will be overcorrected in a redshift- and spatially-dependent manner following the pattern of the cosmic web.'' Now, whether the CSFD result is more believable than SFD is at the reader's discretion! The saving grace is the star-based systematics tests, whose pretty great results suggest that our analysis isn't completely off. 

The sharp increase in \av at $r < 50$ \hinv kpc for WISExSuperCOSMOS, SDSS, and GSWLC is also interesting. I imagine that region is really the extended disk of the galaxies and probably consists of material stripped from infalling satellites or galactic outflows, as at least some of those galaxies must be star-forming. Conversely, the lack of extinction about redMaGiC LRGs at low impact parameters region jibes with the idea of quiescent galaxies not having a lot of dusty outflows. I am open to other interpretations, though.

In a similar vein, we know that the redMaGiC galaxies are biased tracers of LSS (by biased here I mean have a high galaxy bias relative to some field galaxy). RedMaGiC LRG extinction persisting well into the two-halo regime, where the WISExSuperCOSMOS extinction is tapering off, tells a cool story about redMaGiC galaxies as tracers of LSS, while WISExSuperCOSMOS galaxies are a little more dispersed.

\section{Appendix: Weighted least-squares fit and results}\label{appd:wls}

The important parts of \texttt{do\_WLS\_fit.py}, are reproduced below. 
This code performs a weighted least-squares linear regression to data, taking care to 
transform the variables appropriately if we are fitting a power law..

\small

\begin{verbatim}
"""
Inputs
    this_dk: should be the `compensated' treecorr output
    dust_rel: the corrected A_V relationship (dk-dr+rr)
    name: what are we calling it?
    start: exclude points with index < 'start' for fitting
    end: ibidem, but for points with index > 'end'
    log: if True, fit the relationship in log-space to stabilize the fits.
         Default is False.
"""

## If doing a logarithmic fit, transform the 
## dependent variable (the fully compensated TreeCorr kappa)
if log == True:
   # Keep positive values only
   ok = (dust_rel > 0)
   dust_rel = dust_rel[ok]
   this_dk = this_dk[ok]
   
   # Fitting log of dust relation, so take log
   Y = np.log(dust_rel)
   
   ## Also need to transform the weights!
   weights_OG = 1/(this_dk[`sigma']**2)
   #weight_scale = (1/Y)**2
   weight_scale = (1/(Y+1))**2
   weights = weights_OG * weight_scale

## If not doing a logarithmic fit, don't transform
else:
   Y = dust_rel
   weights = 1/(this_dk[`sigma']**2)

## If plotting X on a log-scale, which we are, 
## fit relationship on log scale
X = this_dk['meanlogr']

## Add intercept to abscissa
X = sm.add_constant(X)

## Do fit
mod_wls = sm.WLS(Y, X, weights=weights)
res_wls = mod_wls.fit()

## Print results
print(res_wls.summary())
\end{verbatim}

cat = Table.read(`GSWLC-X2_in_SDSS.fits')

# Filter out objects with flag_sed !=0 and also do a redshift cut
keep = (cat[`z'] < 0.18) & (cat[`flag_sed'] == 0)
cat = cat[keep]


# Calculate specific star formation rate (sSFR) 
# and elimate those with sSFR == 0
sSFR = np.power(cat[`log_SFRsed'],10) / np.power(cat[`log_M_star'], 10)
nonzero = (sSFR != 0)
cat = cat[nonzero]; sSFR = sSFR[nonzero]

# Add sSFR column column for convenience
cat.add_column(np.log10(sSFR), name='log10_sSFR')

# Select the quiescent galaxies
# These selections are based on Salim et al. 2018 criteria
ok_for_passive = (
    (cat[`UV_survey'] == 2) | (cat[`UV_survey'] == 3)
    ) 
    & (np.log10(sSFR) < -11.5)
passive = cat[ok_for_passive]

# Select star-forming!
star_forming = (np.log10(sSFR) > -11.5)
sf_gals = cat[star_forming]

# Write catalogs to file
passive.write(`gswlc_quiescent_galaxies.fits', overwrite=True)
sf_gals.write(`gswlc_sf_galaxies.fits', overwrite=True)
\end{verbatim}
\normalsize


%%%%
\subsection{Stellar mass and star-forming/quiescent profiles}
%%%%

As a reminder, these are the results of cross-correlating the GSWLC-X2 foreground catalog with the SDSS background galaxy catalog. At low impact parameters, the star-forming galaxies have twice as much extinction as the quiescent galaxies, in line with expectations. At larger impact parameters, the trend is opposite to what one might expect from Figure \ref{fig:redmagicfits}: the quiescent galaxies have little dust, while the star-forming galaxies have dust well into the two-halo regime. Before reading too much into this difference, it should be noted that the SFD98 extinction correction was used for all profiles here. Based on the CSFD vs. SFD98 results shown in Figure \ref{fig:csfdvsnot}, I expect that adopting CSFD would increase the amplitude of the signal for quiescent galaxies and decrease the signal of star-forming galaxies. This will be the subject of future exploration!
